Additionally, this platform is being developed independently by an external developer with no employment or associative ties to the club. The intention is to present this project as a proposal to the current club management upon completion.

# 1. Centralized Enforcement Regime (RCE)

Established by Law No. 14,193/2021 (SAF Law), Articles 10 and 13–17.

- **Mechanism:** Concentrates all civil and labor enforcement proceedings predating the SAF into a single centralized court, which receives and distributes funds in an orderly manner among creditors.
- **Mandatory allocation:** At least 20% of the SAF’s monthly operating revenues, plus 50% of any dividends, interest on equity, or other compensation owed by the SAF to the shareholder club/association.
- **Term:** 6 years, extendable by an additional 4 years, provided that at least 60% of the original liabilities are paid during the initial period—allowing up to 10 years of total breathing room.
- **Creditor priority order (Article 17):** Elderly individuals, people with serious illnesses, wage claims below 60 minimum wages, pregnant women, and workplace accident victims receive priority over other creditors.
- **Practical advantage over Judicial Recovery (RJ):** The RCE does not require a General Creditors’ Meeting for approval under the same procedures as Law No. 11,101/2005, and has already been used by clubs that were not yet formally SAFs, such as Corinthians, Santos, and Vasco da Gama—particularly relevant for Tubarão, which operates under a hybrid structure (SAF plus an association that still retains participation).
- **Labor-law precedent:** The predecessor mechanism to the RCE (the "Labor Act") was successfully used by Flamengo and Guarani, which eliminated their labor liabilities. Vasco da Gama, however, failed to maintain payments and ultimately faced forced enforcement proceedings. This demonstrates that the regime only works with genuine cash-flow discipline and is not an automatic solution.
- **Recent update (important addition):** A bill modernizing the SAF Law was approved by the Chamber of Deputies and sent for presidential approval in 2026. The bill makes monthly allocations mandatory (preventing SAFs from concentrating payments only in the final year of the six-year period), expands the calculation base for the 20% allocation to include all monthly revenue except financial income, and requires SAFs to publicly disclose shareholder meeting minutes and detailed ownership structures. The presidential approval process should be monitored before finalizing Tubarão’s legal framework.

# 2. Judicial Recovery (RJ)

- **General mechanism:** Approval through a General Creditors’ Meeting (AGC), with the possibility of grace periods (_standstill_), interest freezes, debt-to-equity conversions, and principal reductions (haircuts).
- **Joinville EC (correction to the original information):** The recovery plan approved in 2023 did not establish a 20-year repayment period. The Court of Justice of Santa Catarina granted two years, starting in May 2023, for creditor payments. For Tubarão, the key takeaway is not the duration but the methodology: the monthly payment was recalculated based on the club’s actual financial capacity, reducing it from BRL 181,800 to BRL 40,000 per month. This principle of "aligning obligations with real cash flow" is the lesson worth replicating.
- **Paraná Clube (adjustment to the original information):** The approved haircut was not a single 40% figure. It varied by creditor class, reaching 11.5%, 38.5%, and 49.8% depending on whether creditors were labor, unsecured, or small-business creditors, reducing total debt from BRL 123 million to just over BRL 60 million. Regarding the club headquarters: it was not "shielded" from creditors. The property already benefited from municipal legal protections due to its original donation for sports purposes, but the club leveraged that protection by **offering the headquarters as collateral** to unlock the plan and attract SAF investors. In 2025, creditors challenged the arrangement again, seeking rights to part of the property sale proceeds—highlighting that asset-backed guarantees frequently generate litigation and require carefully drafted agreements.

# 3. Membership Program (Sócio-Torcedor)

- Base communication on belonging, emotional connection, and civic/community engagement—not sporting performance.
- Adopt radical transparency (see Item 7 – Governance) as a prerequisite for credibility.
- Marketing message: "Rescue operation—you are the owner of the rebuilding process."
- Identity-related benefits: digital membership card, inclusion on a physical monument, voting rights, and advisory polls.
- Utility benefits: access to home matches and a simple welcome kit.
- Corporate membership plans (companies acting as sustaining members).
- Public and visible goals ("X members fund the salary of one youth academy category").
- **Addition:** Linking voting rights to a minimum membership tenure (e.g., one year, as practiced by Unionistas de Salamanca—see Item 7) helps prevent organized groups from capturing the decision-making process and provides governance stability from the outset.

# 4. Matchday Revenue Innovation

- Close part of the stadium and concentrate spectators in a single section to reduce operating costs and improve the perceived atmosphere.
- Create a civic pre-game plaza featuring food trucks and local craft breweries, with a small percentage of sales allocated to the club.
- Establish a Fan Zone with local bands and social projects.
- Halftime activations and fan engagement activities.
- Group and family ticket packages.
- Photo sessions with players.
- **Addition:** Consider a monthly "Kids Day" where children's admission is tied to the purchase of an adult membership. This encourages family attendance and creates the next generation of members with minimal acquisition cost.

# 5. Barter Agreements and Academic Partnerships

- Negotiate advertising in exchange for goods and services from local businesses.
- Establish academic partnerships through scholarships and research projects.
- **Addition:** Educational institutions in the region (in Tubarão’s case, Unisul is the primary local reference) often have extension and research programs in management, sports marketing, and physical education that can be offered as low-cost partnerships for the club. This may include supervised interns instead of direct hires, as well as undergraduate and graduate research projects using the club as a case study, generating additional media exposure at no cost.

# 6. Brand Repositioning

- Build an underdog narrative centered on reconstruction—a club wounded by its past and returning to the embrace of the community "one brick at a time."
- Position community redemption as the primary emotional trigger.
- Sell local business leaders on the idea of becoming "saviors of a cultural asset of the city," rather than simply purchasing advertising space.
- Monetize naming rights (stadium, youth academy, training facilities).
- **Addition:** This narrative is only sustainable if paired with the radical transparency outlined in Item 7. Redemption stories without visible accountability tend to lose credibility quickly among more skeptical stakeholders, particularly business leaders who may have suffered losses in the past.

# 7. Governance

- **Unionistas de Salamanca (Spain):** Founded in 2013 by supporters following the judicial dissolution of Unión Deportiva Salamanca, the club is democratically managed by its members under the principle of "one member, one vote," following the model of clubs such as FC United of Manchester and AFC Wimbledon. The club now has more than 5,430 members—a historic record—and closed its most recent fiscal year with an accumulated surplus of more than €80,000, demonstrating that the principle of "never spending more than you earn" can be operationally viable even in professional competition.
- Bylaw reform, financial statement disclosure, live-streamed assemblies, and direct member voting.
- **Real Oviedo (Spain):** A dispersed ownership model among thousands of small shareholders—already detailed previously—currently maintaining more than 18,000 members/shareholders.
- **Addition:** For Tubarão, where the SAF and association still coexist, it would be prudent to formalize—before any new fundraising effort—a member advisory council with veto power over strategic asset decisions (asset sales, naming rights agreements, new investors). This reduces the political and legal risk of repeating the current governance challenges (a majority investor without sufficient funding capacity and an immobilized association) while also serving as a credibility signal to attract the small-scale contributions described in Item 3.
